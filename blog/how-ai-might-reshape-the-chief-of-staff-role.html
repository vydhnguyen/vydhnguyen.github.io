How AI Might Reshape the Chief of Staff Role

March 16, 2026

The Chief of Staff role has always been defined by proximity to decision-making - sitting at the intersection of information flow, prioritization, and organizational coordination.

As AI tools mature, two dynamics are converging. First, many tasks historically owned by CoS functions - meeting synthesis, status tracking, cross-functional communication coordination - are becoming partially automatable. Second, the cognitive surface area that leaders must manage is expanding, which may increase the demand for skilled human judgment.

What Changes

AI excels at aggregating information and producing first drafts. A CoS who previously spent hours synthesizing briefings from across departments can now redirect that energy toward interpretation and recommendation. The raw synthesis becomes a commodity; the editorial judgment remains scarce.

Similarly, tracking mechanisms - project dashboards, risk registers, decision logs - can increasingly be populated by AI agents watching source data. The CoS role shifts from maintenance of these systems to design of them.

This is a meaningful shift. Designing a coordination system requires understanding what information matters, who needs it, and in what form. That design judgment is deeply contextual and depends on organizational knowledge that AI does not possess.

What Stays the Same

The human dynamics of the role do not compress well. Navigating relationships between senior leaders, reading the subtext of organizational friction, and knowing when not to surface an issue - these remain deeply contextual skills.

Trust, too, is irreplaceable. A Chief of Staff earns authority through demonstrated discretion and judgment over time. No AI produces that legitimacy. The people in rooms making consequential decisions want a trusted human interlocutor, not an automated summary of what was discussed.

A Reframing

Rather than asking whether AI threatens the CoS role, a more useful question is: what does the role look like when the CoS has an AI collaborator available at all times?

The answer is likely a function that operates at higher leverage - fewer hours on data assembly, more hours on synthesis, judgment, and stakeholder navigation. The best Chiefs of Staff will be those who learn to orchestrate AI tools as part of their operating system, freeing attention for the work that requires genuine human presence.

This is not a story about displacement. It is a story about reallocation of effort toward the work that matters most.

This essay reflects personal observations and is not intended as a prediction.
